A Hack for Job Applicants4/15/2014

I'll be upfront; I'm currently unemployed and looking for work. Searching for a job is a pain, and the process of crafting a customized cover letter for each application is tedious. This process drains time from what you really should be doing while unemployed; building your skills. So I wanted to build a web application that helps manage this process. Unfortunately, my ambitions far exceed my skills. Therefore, I am likely to break the discussion of this project into multiple posts, as my development will be iterative. Also, it will alow me to explore in greater detail some of the challenges I'm facing.

The first problem I want to solve is relatively simple. Suppose I am in the market for an Actuarial Analayst position (I pick this example because I'm familiar with it). Let's further assume that the content of my cover letter does not change for each application, as the job descriptions are relatively uniform. In this case, I only need to change the company and position titles mentioned in my cover letter. My program will accept three arguments: cover_letter, current_job_details, new_job_details. It takes an exisiting cover_letter, and replaces the current_job_details with the new_job_details (position, company). So this program saves me some time creating the multiple documents I need. All I need is a cover letter (.txt), and a list of jobs I've found myself. Cool.

But what happens when jobs descriptions vary? Perhaps this would motivate you to change your cover letter to better suit the position. Suppose one class of jobs requires 'pension' experience, while another class requires 'pricing' experience. Assume you have both. Your cover letter should be modified to discuss the relevant experience. Our simple program above would need major modifications to accomodate this caveat. We no longer require one cover letter, but a set of 'experiences' that can be interchanged. Luckily, cover letters have structure. Let's start with a simple example.

Suppose my cover_letter has three paragraphs; intro, experience, conclusion. Continuing from above, one set of applications will be of the form (intro, pension_experience, conclusion) while the other will be of the form (intro, pricing_experience, conclusion). In this case, I no longer have a cover letter. I have a structure which takes 3 arguments (intro, experience, conclusion) to produce a cover letter.

Under these circumstances, parsing cover letters becomes a critical component of the application. The text in a cover letter needs to be mapped to a predefined structure (intro, experience, conclusion). How can you tell what text goes where? Are paragraphs a good proxy for splitting a document? What if the cover letter has more (or less) than 3 paragraphs? Things begin to fall apart.

We can't directly parse any given cover letter to a predefined structure (intro, experience, conclusion) because not every user will follow that convention. But we can parse a cover letter in another way; paragraphs, sentences, words. Now, the user needs to identify which elements are variant, and how text will ultimately be grouped to accord with our (intro, experience, conclusion) structure. See below:

  • single words ("Sunlife" => "Manulife")
  • single words ("Excel" => "GGY Axis")
  • sentences ("I am applying to work at Sunlife." => "I would love to join Sunlife.")
  • sentences ("I have 5 years experience in Excel." => "I work daily in Excel.")
  • paragraphs ("I am applying to work at Sunlife. I have 5 years experience in Excel." => "I would love to join Manulife. I work daily in GGY Axis."

Now we're starting to see how a cover letter might be modified to correspond to a particular job posting. Notice above how each word, sentence, or paragraph can be further classified. Sunlife and Manulife are Companies. The sentences describe what you're applying to and your relevant experience. So I'm hopeful that I can cobble something together that produces an application that resonates with the job posting.

One last preface. In no way do I mean to imply a 'higher than though' tone in this epitome. "I have a lot of beliefs and I live by none of [them]. I just like believing them. They make me feel good about who I am. But if they get in the way of a thing I want, I do that." - Louis C.K.

  • Your salary may not reflect your efforts. Don't let this bother you. "Be so good they can't ignore you." - Martin. Corrollary: You're not as good as you think you are.
  • Don't give up (or give up, whatever). It took me 4 attempts to pass exam C. It hurt what little remained of my ego. But, your opportunities (and salary) improve if you get credentialed. Your FSA is like an international green card. Care to work in Europe, the US, Hong Kong? You can do that!
  • It's a pretty sweet job. The profession is far from perfect. But, relatively speaking, it is sufficiently rewarding. You will encounter sloppy processes, bad bosses, and long hours. Do your best to improve your situation within your means. But, your circumstance is *likely* better than 90% of what else is available to you.

There you have it; the actuarial survival guide. I hope it provides some relief in the trenches of the workplace. But I should be honest. I'm more interested in hearing about what you're up to. The Economics of Household Chores4/1/2014

Every month, parents post a list of chores that they'd like their children to get done. Each chore is given an initial price. Each child can bid on any number of chores they wish to complete. Parents want all chores completed. How should the parents allocate their total cost amongst all chores that will induce their children to bid on the full list? This is the problem I want to solve. We're going to devise an algorithm that induces 'full coverage' by adjusting relative prices in the event chore(s) have no bids.

On adjusting relative prices: When we increase the price of one chore, we lower the price of another, such that total cost remains fixed. Varying total cost to induce 'full coverage' (hey kids, $10,000/month for chores?) certainly works. But we assume total cost is set sufficiently high that 'full coverage', if not present, can be achieved by finding the right price distribution. In the event the algorithm fails to induce 'full coverage', our view is the parents are cheap, the children formed a cartel, or there is one nasty ass chore on that list.

Anyway, to dicuss how we might construct this algorithm, it is best to illustrate this with an example. The syntax below is consistent with how you would build a dictionary in python.

  • choreA = {:price => '$12', :bids => 2, :description => 'dishes'}
  • choreB = {:price => '$5', :bids => 0, :description => 'garbage'}
  • choreC = {:price => '$8', :bids => 3, :description => 'walk dog'}
  • chores = {:total => '$25', :coverage => false}
  • kids = [:sally=>['dishes' 'walk dog'], :jessie=>['walk dog'], :raphael=>['dishes', 'walk dog']
    •

No one wants to do the garbage, and everyone wants to walk the dog. How might prices adjust? Naturally, garbage increases and walking the dog decreases. Notice that dishes also has 2 bids. We could adjust this price downward and add the difference to garbage. How's this.

  1. var adustment = 5%
  2. Let X = [choreA, choreB, choreC].
  3. Reduce X[i][:price] by (X.min - X[i])*(adjustment) for all i in X.
  4. Add the cumulative reduction in prices to the chore with the minimum number of bids.
  5. Re-open the bidding.
  6. Are all chores covered? If true, stop here.
    If false, go to next step.
  7. set adustment = adjustment - 1%
  8. Go back to Step 1

Applying this algorithm, the dictionary transforms in the following way:

  • choreA = {:price => '$10.80', :bids => 1, :description => 'dishes'}
  • choreB = {:price => '$7.40', :bids => 2, :description => 'garbage'}
  • choreC = {:price => '$6.80', :bids => 3, :description => 'walk dog'}
  • chores = {:total => '$25', :coverage => true}
  • kids = [:sally=>['dishes', 'garbage', 'walk dog'], :jessie=>['walk dog'], :raphael=>['garbage', 'walk dog']

Dishes and walking the dog prices fell (by 10% and 15% respectively), while the garbage increased (by 48%). We were able to get all the chores done, without having to increase our total cost (which remained at $25). Cool. Now we have a method for adjusting prices to achieve 'full coverage'. In the event 'full coverage' is not achieved after the first time round, run the algorithm again. Implicitly, the algorithm is programmed to stop after 5 iterations. This is due to the scaling down of our adjustment factor from (initially set at 5%) in 1% increments.

So what about the app!? Well, once you have an algorithm for dynamically adjusting prices, and assigning chores (more on that later) kids can then track their data over time. For example, they could answer - How much am I earning each month? How much relative to my siblings? What percentage of the available total income did I receive? What chores did I complete? Which chores earned me the most relative income? What combination of chores would I need to do to earn $X? And the gamification? Here are some of my ideas:

  • Falling Prices: If there's a chore that needs urgent attention, it's price drops over time. Motivates early completion.
  • Blind Auction: Each child submits a price, and the chore is assigned to the lowest bidder. A child motivated to buy something might be aggressive in their pricing strategy against their siblings. This only works if the cumulative bids do not exceed the total cost set by the parents.
  • Bonus: A $10 bonus is embedded in a particular chore. The bonus is only revealed after chores have been assigned. This can induce broader bidding in each category, but effectively raises the total cost of chores that week.
  • More Chores!: Allow your children to propose additional chores they wish to complete. Choose to accept, reject, or counter offer.

Expectation in a Sublet Lottery3/26/2014

The big picture. I'm interested in building a model that generates probabilities of drawing a room in a sublet lottery in python. This is motivated by my experience at Bitmaker Labs in Toronto. The company leases property to sublet to students from out of town. However, DEMAND >> SUPPLY. Therefore, understanding the likelihood of drawing a room from Bitmaker Labs will influence the decision to look for property elsewhere. Bitmaker Labs is currently piloting a rental arrangement with one condo with 4 rooms.

Our example. 17 students need housing. Only 4 rooms (A,B,C,D) are available. Each room is slightly different, and are thus offered at different prices ($2,000, $2,375, $2,875, $3,375). Every individual is allowed to bid on any number of the rooms with a binary indicator (yes/no). Therefore, it is legitimate to have Jon request A and B, and Jen to request C and B, and Tina request B, C, and D. But they may be assigned at most one room. Our example is illustrated below:

Note: A (non-randomly) generated input with the following condition: if an individual bids on more than one room, the bids must be adjacent (i.e you can bid on A&B, not A&D, unless A&B&C&D). This will likely not hold in practice, but it produces some interesting results, and guides our post mortem discussion and analysis.

If we draw with replacement, computing the probability of being assigned a room is relatively straight forward. Simply:

1 - ( 1-P(A) )*( 1-P(B) )*( 1-P(C) )*( 1-P(D) )

The table below is meant to give some insight into an individual's sensitivity to price, and how demand is distributed accross product categories. Note the difference in price between Room A and Room D; it's $1,375. If individuals are price sensitive we should expect to see fewer people bidding on Room A and Room D (A&D), than say, Room A and Room B (A&B), where the differential is smaller (only $375). If you look at the 'Counts' table below, you'll notice that A&D < A&B => Pr(A&D) < Pr(A&B). What might we expect it there are individuals with 'high end' tastes? Ramblings on Financial "Advice"3/3/2014

I'm in the middle of wrapping up my studies at Bitmaker Labs, and deciding how best to put all the information we've been presented on modern web development to use after this whole thing wraps up. I've long been interested in financial markets by way of my work (Manulife), and I suppose, how the 'average' person makes sense of the plethora of personal financial advice. According to CBC, almost a third of Canadians rely on professional financial advisors to guide their decisions with respect to managing debt, a mortgage, education and retirement savings. And it turns out those many of them are not that good. While there has been some headway leveraging modern technologies (web apps, mobile apps) to better inform consumers of financial products, it's my humble opinion that we still have a long way to go. So that's my starting point.

There's a database of financial professionals registered in Canada held online at Canadian Securities Administrators (CSA). It holds records, both active and historical, of 290,000 registered investment professionals, as well as their location, employer, phone, title, tenure, credentials and disciplinary action. According to their website, they are "Securities regulators from each of the 10 provinces and 3 territories in Canada have teamed up to form the Canadian Securities Administrators (CSA). The CSA protects Canadian investors from unfair, improper, or fraudulent practices and fosters fair and efficient capital markets." The database is hard to interact with, and looks ripe for some scraping and reconstitution.

A major naivite of mine entering this course was the notion that "learning to program" is the same as "programming". It's not. Put another way, there is an enormous amount of programming, software, or techniques that you are required to learn in the process of completing a project. So while Bitmaker Labs focuses largely on the Ruby language, I've come accross many a situation that demands an alternative method. Case in point, iMacros web scripting abilities are far better suited to traversing and saving this CSA database in .txt files than a ruby gem or methodology. Am I familiar with iMacros syntax, potential, and particularities? No. But I've been able to piece together just enough functionality to perform the function I need. It's good enough for me, and it should be good enough for you too.

Dictated by the current structure, the iMacro script will need to traverse 290,000 pages to scrape all available details on active/historical financial advisors. It's unfortunate, but the CSA offers no way to search for active users only. Alas. Once I've left this script running for about 28 hours (8 seconds per record, you do the math), I should have all the info I need. Then RUBY can do its thing. That is, I'll be using Ruby to parse the .txt files (but not to create them).

One of the characteristics of the .txt files being created is that each contains only one user record, and this record tends to start at line 370 (spot checking confirmation). The status (active, historical) is available on line 380 (again, spot checked). Since we are only interested in constructing a database on active financial advisors, ostensibly we can divide our text records into two groups: active and historical. Once complete, we can create a mega text document with only active users, containing all that wondeful information that we can then investigate. How many active advisors are there? How many in Pickering? How many have been disciplined? How many are male? How many are credentialed (CFA, CA, CFP)? How many work for RBC? Dundee Wealth? Exciting stuff.

Because you can write javascript in your iMacro, you can create loops where otherwise the instructions would be explicit. In my case, I need to paginate through 2,900 pages (100 records/page). This is well suited to a loop, and I am currently investigating ways to best write this. I suppose I'll keep you up to date on my progress. Don't hold your breath. You might pass out.
